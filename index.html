Master's Thesis, H2 2026

Digital Pathology Research

Deep Learning Detection of SARIFA in Colorectal Cancer Whole-Slide Images

Tile classification · Multiple-instance learning · Weakly supervised WSI analysis · Tumor microenvironment

SARIFA (Stroma AReactive Invasion Front Areas) is a histomorphologic feature defined by direct contact between tumor cell clusters and adipose tissue at the invasion front, without the expected desmoplastic stromal barrier. SARIFA-positivity is an independent prognostic marker in colorectal and other gastrointestinal cancers, linked to altered immune microenvironment and tumor–adipocyte metabolic reprogramming. Current pathologist assessment is manual, subjective, and not scalable to large cohorts.

My thesis builds a staged, contract-driven pipeline for slide-level SARIFA detection across the TCGA colorectal cancer cohort (~200 patients, ~250 GB of whole-slide images). The emphasis is on rigorous ML engineering: modelling decisions are pre-declared, splits are fixed and patient-disjoint, and the held-out test set is opened exactly once per model. The tile classifier reaches AUROC 0.985 across five seeds, and the current patient-level baseline (non-parametric top-k aggregation) reaches test AUROC 0.849, AUPRC 0.821. A learned multiple-instance learning stage is in progress. A separate final test set of Finnish CRC patients is reserved for the final locked evaluation pass.

Aim 01

Tile classifier

ResNet-50 trained to classify 512 × 512 px H&E tiles as SARIFA-positive or negative. Three-zone label policy with POS (ROI overlap), IGNORE (boundary band), and NEG (negative slides only) prevents false-negative injection from incompletely annotated positive slides. Focal loss, balanced sampling, and post-hoc temperature calibration.

Aim 02

Patient-level aggregation

Non-parametric top-k pooling as the locked baseline, followed by attention-based MIL (ABMIL) with a partial instance supervision head. The instance head is trained on annotated POS tiles and confirmed-NEG tiles. High-scoring ambiguous tiles identified in pathologist review are excluded from instance loss rather than forced to zero, preserving true signal outside annotation boundaries.

Aim 03

Spatial interpretability

Calibrated score heatmaps over full WSI thumbnails, ROI-contact enrichment analysis, and spatial geometry of the high-scoring tile subset including boundary proximity and connected-component span, Spearman correlation between slide score and distance to annotated invasion front (ρ = 0.68, p = 0.00069). Attention maps from the MIL stage extend this toward a learned localization signal.

Pipeline

202 TCGA-CRC whole-slide images, 69 SARIFA-positive, patient-disjoint 70/15/15 split stratified by ROI yield
4.2 million manifest-backed tiles from QuPath GeoJSON through Shapely to 3-zone label assignment with micron-calibrated boundary bands
ResNet-50 tile classifier with focal loss (α 0.25, γ 2.0), AdamW, balanced 1:1 sampling, and AUROC 0.985 ± 0.001 across five seeds
Full-manifest WSI inference to calibrated score parquet, with temperature scaling fitted on class-balanced validation tiles
Top-k aggregation (locked k=1) gives test AUROC 0.849 and AUPRC 0.821. ABMIL with instance supervision is in progress
Attention map outputs per patient for spatial comparison with annotated ROI geometry and heatmap overlays

What I care about

Pre-declared contracts. Evaluation metrics, thresholds, and locked decisions are written before results are seen, not reverse-engineered from them
Audit trail through versioned parquet artefacts with SHA-256 hashes, staged run directories, and reproducible splits
Failure modes first, with Gate A (Grad-CAM), Gate B (pathologist tile review), and explicit documentation of marginal gate passes and known limitations
Slice-aware evaluation with TSS site monitoring, per-split label counts, and documented val-test AUROC gaps

About Me

Beyond the Work

I care about correctness more than cleverness. I'd rather build a simple, well-validated pipeline I can defend in a methods section than an elaborate one I can't fully explain. That instinct comes from scientific training, where you're accountable for your methodology, not just your results, and it carries into every engineering problem I work on.

What I find most interesting is working at the boundary between disciplines: where biological questions meet computational methods, where a model's output has to mean something to a clinician, where the right answer requires understanding both sides. That is the kind of work I have been building toward and where I am most useful.

The same instinct carries outside work. I'd rather ski a long trail through forest than a lifeless slope, ride proper bush on a mountain bike, and find the good coffee in a city before the obvious one. I read widely in history, philosophy, and science because the most useful ideas rarely stay in their original field.
